# ADR-002: Prioritize visible automatic recovery with a lossless fallback

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-07-20

## Context

Iterative coding users interrupt an active agent turn to refine, narrow, or replace their instruction. In the current experience, a first-turn interruption can leave the session at **Safe start unavailable**, with the next draft unsent and `/new` as the only recovery route. That breaks the user's working thread at the point where fast correction is most valuable.

The approved product direction already limits recovery to one continuation after an explicit Hard Stop and never sends into an uncertain session. PRD clarification established the remaining user-facing priorities: users want to see that a safe continuation is queued and will continue automatically; if safety cannot be proven, they want the draft preserved with a clear `/new` action; and broader rollout must be earned through lossless-race and privacy evidence rather than immediate provider parity.

## Decision

V1 will present Hard Stop recovery as a visible, automatic continuation experience for a safely settled session. It will communicate that exactly one continuation is queued and will become the next ordinary turn when the session is confirmed safe. Users can continue editing a later draft, and a second Escape returns the queued continuation to the composer without pretending it was sent.

When safety is uncertain, V1 will preserve the continuation visibly and offer `/new` as the explicit recovery path. It will not create a replacement session automatically, request a second confirmation for a safely queued continuation, or expose an implementation-level failure explanation as a prerequisite for recovery.

The feature's expansion gate is evidence: no lost drafts, duplicate dispatches, or continuation-content leaks across its defined interruption scenarios. Provider parity and broader recovery capabilities remain later decisions.

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Require manual resubmission after every interruption

- **Description**: Return the draft after settlement and require the user to send it again.
- **Pros**: Familiar interaction and a smaller immediate behavioral change.
- **Cons**: Leaves users responsible for detecting settlement and does not repair the promised same-session continuation flow.
- **Why rejected**: It preserves text but not momentum, which is the central product value for iterative coding users.

### Alternative 2: Start a replacement session automatically on uncertainty

- **Description**: Move the draft into a new session without requiring an explicit `/new` decision.
- **Pros**: Could shorten recovery in some failure cases.
- **Cons**: Changes session context without a deliberate user choice and can obscure why continuity was unavailable.
- **Why rejected**: The user selected an explicit, visible fallback that retains control and avoids pretending uncertainty is recoverable automatically.

### Alternative 3: Delay V1 until provider-parity coverage is available

- **Description**: Withhold the experience until every supported provider can offer the same continuation result.
- **Pros**: A simpler cross-provider product message.
- **Cons**: Prolongs the known interruption failure for users who can be served safely now and turns a narrow reliability repair into a broad certification program.
- **Why rejected**: V1 can state its safety boundary honestly, preserve drafts everywhere, and expand only with evidence.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Gives iterative users a clear and low-friction way to correct course without losing the live conversation.
- Makes ambiguity honest and recoverable: unsent text remains under the user's control.
- Defines safety and privacy outcomes as the release gate, protecting trust before coverage expands.

### Negative

- Some interruptions will still require `/new` when continuity cannot be proven.
- The experience must communicate queued, sent, and restored states clearly enough that users never infer an action that did not occur.
- Provider coverage may expand unevenly until evidence supports a broader promise.

### Risks

- **Users mistake a queued continuation for a sent message** — use plain, visible status and preserve the editable composer context.
- **Users perceive `/new` as data loss** — keep the continuation visibly recoverable and describe the fallback as a safety protection.
- **A narrow release does not produce enough learning** — measure only local, opt-in, content-free outcomes and revisit expansion after the defined evidence gate.
