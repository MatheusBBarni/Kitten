# ADR-002: Make Every Update Outcome Self-Describing and Fail Closed

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-07-20

## Context

The channel-preserving update idea establishes that Kitten must never change an installation it cannot positively identify. Product clarification for the PRD resolves the remaining user-experience decisions: an ambiguous invocation must receive both supported recovery paths; verified attempts must identify the installation channel and version outcome; and later expansion depends on proven trust rather than usage alone.

The product must give developers a reliable maintenance action without suggesting that any executable named `kitten` is safe to update. A terse success signal or a likely-but-unproven recovery command would shift that uncertainty back to the user.

## Decision

V1 treats `kitten --update` as a self-describing trust action.

- A verified global npm or verified standalone installation receives a clear progress/result experience that names its channel and reports the prior and resulting version, or that it is already current.
- An unsupported or inconclusive installation remains untouched, exits nonzero, states that it could not be updated safely, and presents both supported recovery commands without guessing the user's channel.
- The command remains explicit, latest-stable-only, and independent from Cockpit launch, repository state, agent availability, prompts, and relaunch.
- V2+ expansion is gated first on zero unsafe mutations or channel switches, then on evidence of successful verified use and lower manual-update support burden.

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Suggest only the most likely recovery channel

- **Description**: Infer the likely npm or standalone origin and show one corresponding reinstall command.
- **Pros**: Shorter output and less apparent choice for the developer.
- **Cons**: Turns unproven inference into product guidance and can send users down the wrong recovery path.
- **Why rejected**: The feature's purpose is trustworthy maintenance; both supported paths are safer and more honest than a guess.

### Alternative 2: Report only a generic success or failure

- **Description**: Keep update output terse and rely on exit codes for detail.
- **Pros**: Minimal copy and a conventional CLI surface.
- **Cons**: Leaves developers unable to tell which channel acted, whether an already-current result is normal, or how to recover safely.
- **Why rejected**: Channel and version visibility are central to user confidence and supportability.

### Alternative 3: Broaden immediately to automated update management

- **Description**: Add background checks, notifications, automatic retry, and more distribution channels in the first release.
- **Pros**: More convenience and apparent coverage.
- **Cons**: Expands the trust and lifecycle surface before the core promise is proven.
- **Why rejected**: The chosen V1 is intentionally surgical; safety evidence must precede convenience expansion.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Developers can distinguish a verified update, an already-current result, and a safe refusal without inspecting local state.
- The same no-guessing rule governs both execution and recovery guidance.
- Product expansion has an explicit evidence threshold instead of growing from anecdote or convenience pressure.

### Negative

- Some legitimate but unusual installations will require manual reinstallation.
- Result output is more detailed than a minimal silent updater.
- V1 deliberately leaves some user convenience unmet.

### Risks

- **Safe refusal is misread as a malfunction** — clearly state that no change was made and make both recovery commands copyable.
- **Users overestimate the trust guarantee** — accurately describe checksum verification and avoid claiming protection against publisher compromise.
- **Future scope expands before confidence exists** — require zero trust incidents before considering more channels or automation.

## Implementation Notes

- Preserve the documented two installation channels and their explicit recovery commands.
- Use consistent outcome wording in CLI help, README, and release guidance.
