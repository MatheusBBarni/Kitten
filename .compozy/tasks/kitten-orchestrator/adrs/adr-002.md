# ADR-002: Make Phase 1 an invisible Cockpit parity release

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-07-16

## Context

The approved idea proposes a two-application Kitten product, starting with a
Cockpit workspace conversion before the desktop Orchestrator migration. Fresh
research confirms that developers expect visible, reviewable agent work, but
also expect human-owned approval and merge boundaries. Existing Cockpit users
rely on a stable install, launch, configuration, and reviewed handoff
experience.

The clarification round identified the primary Phase-1 user as an existing
Cockpit developer. That user prioritizes no regression over early access to a
future product family, and selected the complete automated Cockpit contract
matrix as the sole advancement criterion.

## Decision

Make Phase 1 behaviorally invisible to existing Cockpit users. It will preserve
their current install, launch, configuration, and reviewed-handoff experience
without adding a user-facing Orchestrator, Cross-App Handoff, or product-family
preview. The full existing automated Cockpit contract matrix is the required
evidence to advance beyond Phase 1.

Treat the workspace conversion as a trust-preservation release. New user value
from governed unattended work belongs to later, separately scoped phases after
this release has established a reliable baseline.

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Announce the broader product family in Phase 1

- **Description**: Preserve Cockpit while adding user-facing messaging about the
  future two-application product.
- **Pros**: Makes the strategic direction visible and may build anticipation.
- **Cons**: Changes the user-facing release without delivering the promised new
  workflow and creates expectations the phase cannot yet meet.
- **Why rejected**: Existing users chose unchanged behavior as the Phase-1
  promise.

### Alternative 2: Offer an opt-in continuity or Orchestrator preview

- **Description**: Pair the parity release with a small early bridge to the
  future desktop experience.
- **Pros**: Produces earlier learning about cross-app value.
- **Cons**: Mixes a new experience with a trust-preservation release and weakens
  the clarity of the automated parity gate.
- **Why rejected**: The user excluded all new user-facing functionality from
  Phase 1; learning belongs to a later, explicitly bounded phase.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Existing Cockpit users retain a clear, stable product promise.
- Release evidence directly supports the only Phase-1 outcome that matters:
  unchanged behavior.
- Later Orchestrator work starts from a proven baseline rather than a mixed
  migration and feature release.

### Negative

- Phase 1 creates no immediate new workflow for users.
- The broader product direction remains intentionally undiscoverable until a
  later phase is ready to support it.

### Risks

- **Progress appears invisible** — communicate the internal milestone to
  maintainers without changing the user-facing Cockpit workflow.
- **Automated evidence misses perceived regressions** — retain the broad
  contract matrix and document any inherited exceptions rather than declaring
  unqualified parity.
- **Pressure to add previews** — treat any user-facing future-product experience
  as a separate product decision and ADR.

## Implementation Notes

The Phase-1 release must not claim availability of Orchestrator or a new
cross-app workflow. Any release communication must accurately describe the
unchanged Cockpit experience and its evidence.
